Life & Health: Global Life Expectancy in Focus (2000-2015)

A visual story exploring how health, vaccines, and economic factors shape life expectancy around the world.

Global Life Expectancy

At a glance

    From breakthroughs in multiple factors including GDP, vaccination coverage, and health indicators such as infant mortality and HIV incidence, to rising healthcare investments, improvements in life expectancy tell a story of global progress—and persistent inequality.

    Through data-driven visuals, this exploration highlights how health systems, wealth, and social development intertwine to influence how long and how well people live across the world.

Wealth-Health Links

    A higher GDP per capita means a nation and its citizens have more resources to invest in the determinants of health: better nutrition, safe sanitation, advanced medical care, and disease prevention, all of which directly increase life expectancy.

    A healthier population is a more productive population. Fewer work days are lost to illness, children learn better and become a more skilled workforce, and people have longer, more productive working lives, all of which drive economic growth and increase GDP per capita.

Which health factors matter most?

Vaccination coverage and economic status

  • Coverage Levels: Developed countries maintain above 90% coverage for all three vaccines, while developing countries typically range between 75–85%, reflecting gaps in healthcare access and resources.
  • Infrastructure and Access: Well-organized immunization programs in developed nations ensure consistent delivery, whereas developing regions often struggle with limited facilities, supply shortages, and rural inaccessibility.
  • Progress and Challenges: Global initiatives have improved vaccination rates in developing countries, yet conflict, poverty, and misinformation continue to hinder full coverage, keeping the gap between regions significant.
  • Progress and Inequality: Although life expectancy in developing regions has risen significantly over recent decades due to global aid and improved vaccination, economic inequality continues to drive a persistent gap between rich and poor nations.
  • Healthcare Quality: Stronger economies support universal healthcare systems, advanced medical technology, and better disease prevention, contributing to longer lifespans, whereas developing nations face resource constraints and limited medical access.

Fertility vs Life Expectancy: The Development Divide

    Improvements in health and living conditions (leading to higher life expectancy) create the social and economic rationale for having fewer children.

    The subsequent decline in fertility then frees up resources that further improve health, education, and economic well-being, which in turn reinforces both the trend for smaller families and the capacity for longer lives.

Effects of migration and population on life expectancy

  • Positive Net Migration: Countries like the United States and Germany attract large numbers of migrants, often young and working-age individuals, which helps balance aging populations and sustain higher life expectancy through stronger economies and healthcare systems.
  • Negative Net Migration: Nations such as China and India experience more people leaving than entering, often resulting in a loss of skilled workers (“brain drain”) that can limit healthcare capacity and slow improvements in national life expectancy.
  • Demographic Impacts: While high-immigration countries benefit from population diversity and workforce renewal, countries with net emigration may face uneven regional development and aging rural populations, both of which can negatively influence overall life expectancy trends.
  • Economic Scale and Investment: Large economies like the U.S. and China can allocate significant resources to public health, disease prevention, and research, which helps improve life expectancy — though income inequality can still create wide gaps across regions and groups.
  • Population Density and Environment: High population density, particularly in India and parts of China, leads to pollution, overcrowding, and sanitation issues, which can negatively impact public health and reduce life expectancy despite economic progress.

Summary key takeaways

  1. Economic Strength and Health Investment: Countries with higher GDP and stronger economic status can invest more in healthcare, education, and infrastructure, directly contributing to longer life expectancy through better living standards and disease prevention.
  2. Vaccination Coverage and Disease Control: High vaccination rates against diseases like Diphtheria, Hepatitis B, and Polio significantly reduce child mortality and prevent outbreaks, forming a cornerstone for higher average life expectancy, especially in developing regions.
  1. Fertility Rate and Demographic Balance: Lower fertility rates in developed nations often correlate with aging but healthier populations, while high fertility rates in developing countries can strain healthcare systems and lower life expectancy due to limited resources per capita.
  2. Migration and Population Dynamics: Positive net migration (as seen in the U.S. and Germany) introduces younger, healthier populations that support workforce and healthcare sustainability, whereas negative net migration (in countries like India and China) can limit skilled labor retention and slow improvements in life expectancy.
